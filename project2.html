Dr. A.P.J. Abdul Kalam

The Missile Man of India

Avul Pakir Jainulabdeen Abdul Kalam (15 October 1931 â€“ 27 July 2015) was an Indian aerospace scientist and statesman who served as the 11th President of India from 2002 to 2007. Born and raised in Rameswaram, Tamil Nadu, Kalam studied physics and aerospace engineering. He spent the next four decades as a scientist and science administrator, mainly at the Defence Research and Development Organisation (DRDO) and Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO) and was intimately involved in India's civilian space programme and military missile development efforts.

He thus came to be known as the Missile Man of India for his work on the development of ballistic missile and launch vehicle technology. He also played a pivotal organisational, technical, and political role in India's Pokhran-II nuclear tests in 1998, the first since the original nuclear test by India in 1974.

Key Contributions and Achievements:

  • Played a leading role in the development of India's first satellite launch vehicle (SLV-III).
  • Instrumental in the development of various missiles, including Agni and Prithvi.
  • Served as the Chief Scientific Adviser to the Prime Minister and the Secretary of the DRDO from 1992 to 1999.
  • Became a widely popular President of India, known for his humble nature and inspiring speeches.
  • Authored several books, including "Wings of Fire," an autobiography.
  • Recipient of India's highest civilian awards, including the Bharat Ratna.

Dr. Kalam was a visionary who inspired millions, especially the youth, with his emphasis on education, innovation, and self-reliance. His life's journey from a humble background to the highest office in the country is a testament to his dedication and hard work.
